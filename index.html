Underwater caves form through various geological processes over millions of years. The most common type develops in limestone or dolomite regions through a process called dissolution. As slightly acidic groundwater seeps through cracks in the rock, it slowly dissolves the calcium carbonate, enlarging the fissures into caverns. In coastal areas, sea level changes can submerge once-dry caves. Some underwater caves also form from lava tubes created by volcanic activity, while others result from tectonic shifts or the erosive action of waves and currents on softer rock layers. These submerged caverns often feature unique ecosystems and are of great interest to scientists and explorers.
